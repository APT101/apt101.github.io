Policy: Handling Phishing Emails

When you receive a suspicious email, your response matters more than the email itself. This page explains exactly what to do if you think a message might be phishing. The left side is for everyday internet users; the right side is for people using a work or corporate account.

For Home / Personal Accounts

1. When You Receive a Suspicious Email
  • Do not click anything. Don’t click links, buttons, or images. Don’t open attachments.
  • Do not reply to the sender. Don’t argue, don’t ask questions, just ignore them.
  • Check the sender and the message. Look for weird addresses, spelling mistakes, and urgent language.
  • Verify using an official channel. Go directly to the company’s official website or app, or use the phone number listed there.
2. If You Clicked the Link or Opened the Attachment
  • Close the page immediately. If a login page appears, do not type anything.
  • Change your password right away. Do this from the official website or app.
  • If you entered your password: change it on that site and on any other site where you reused it; enable multi-factor authentication.
  • Run an antivirus or security scan. Use your security software to scan your device.
  • Watch your accounts. Check recent activity for anything suspicious.
3. Reporting and Cleaning Up
  • Use your email provider’s “Report phishing” or “Report spam” function if available.
  • Block the sender address if the option exists.
  • Delete the email once you’ve reported or documented it.

For Work / Corporate Accounts

1. When You Receive a Suspicious Email at Work
  • Do not interact with the email. Do not click links, open attachments, or reply.
  • Do not delete it yet. Security teams may need the email for headers, timestamps, and investigation.
  • Report it through the official channel. Use your “Report Phishing” button, forward as attachment to the security mailbox, or open a ticket in the IT/SOC system.
  • Include basic details. Subject line, sender address, and why it seems suspicious (e.g., strange link, unexpected request).
2. If You Clicked, Entered Credentials, or Opened an Attachment
  • Disconnect from the network. Turn off Wi-Fi or unplug the cable.
  • Contact IT or security immediately. Tell them exactly what you clicked, downloaded, or typed.
  • Do not reboot the device unless IT tells you to. Rebooting can remove evidence needed for investigation.
  • Follow IT instructions. This may include forced password resets, session revocations, or re-imaging the machine.
  • If sensitive data might be exposed, inform your manager or incident contact so the organization can follow its incident response process.
3. Ongoing Best Practices at Work
  • Use company-approved email, VPN, and browsers only.
  • Keep your system and software patched and updated.
  • Never forward work data to personal email accounts.
  • Treat unusual requests for payments, gift cards, or data as suspicious until confirmed by phone or in person.
