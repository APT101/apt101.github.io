Glossary: Phishing & Email Security

Plain-language definitions of common phishing and email security terms. This page is meant for non-technical users, so each term is kept short and clear.

Phishing
Fake messages that pretend to be from real companies or people, trying to trick you into clicking links, opening files, or giving away information.
Spear-phishing
Phishing that targets a specific person or small group, often using personal details to look more convincing.
Whaling
Spear-phishing aimed at executives or other high-value targets inside an organization.
Smishing
Phishing that comes through SMS or text messages.
Vishing
Phishing done over voice calls or voicemail, often pretending to be banks, support teams, or government agencies.
Spoofing
Faking an email address, phone number, or website so it looks like it comes from a trusted source.
Malware
Malicious software such as viruses, ransomware, spyware, or trojans that can damage devices or steal information.
Payload
The final harmful action that malware performs once it runs (for example, encrypting files or sending data out).
Credential Harvesting
Stealing usernames, passwords, and other login details, usually through fake login pages.
Social Engineering
Manipulating people using trust, fear, urgency, or curiosity to get them to do something they normally wouldn’t.
MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication)
Using two or more checks to log in, such as a password plus a code from an app or SMS.
Zero-day
A newly discovered software vulnerability that does not yet have a fix or patch.
URL Redirect
A link that sends you to a different website than you expected, often used to hide malicious pages.
Sandboxing
Running a suspicious file in an isolated environment to safely observe what it does.
Threat Intelligence
Information about known attackers, bad domains, IP addresses, and methods used in attacks.
SPF (Sender Policy Framework)
An email record that tells which mail servers are allowed to send messages for a specific domain.
DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)
A system that adds a digital signature to emails so receivers can check that the message wasn’t changed.
DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance)
A policy that tells email receivers what to do when SPF and DKIM checks fail (for example, reject or quarantine the email).
